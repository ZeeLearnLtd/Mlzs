Why Start a School ?

The private Indian education sector has witnessed remarkable growth over the years. In 2023, the market was valued at approximately US$ 48.9 billion and is projected to grow to US$ 125.8 billion by 2032, with a CAGR of 11.1%*. Within this, the K-12 education segment has shown significant expansion, reaching US$ 50.31 billion in 2024 and is anticipated to grow to US$ 89.45 billion by 2030, at a CAGR of 10.13%**. This growth is fueled by India’s increasing population, rising disposable incomes, and a growing demand for quality education.

Key factors contributing to the growth of the Indian education sector include:

Rising Gross Enrolment Ratios (GER) - India’s GER in elementary education reached 100% by 2016 and has continued to grow steadily, ensuring a consistent flow of students across all educational levels. With the growing demand for education, this has bolstered enrolment rates across the country.

Demand for Quality Schools - The rapid growth in India's population and the increasing middle-class segment have escalated the need for quality schools. India requires an estimated 20,000–25,000 high-quality schools (NCERT) to meet the needs of its expanding educational sector. The K-12 segment, valued at US$ 50.31 billion in 2024, is expected to grow to US$ 89.45 billion by 2030, signaling a major shift toward high-quality education**.

Government Support - Policies such as the New Education Policy (NEP) have created a conducive environment for private players to enter the market. The government's push for increased private sector involvement continues to drive innovation, improvement in educational infrastructure, and investments in the sector.

Parental Preference for Private Schools - With over 40% of students vying for spots in just 20% of private schools, the demand for high-quality private education is growing. This preference reflects the increasing desire for world-class facilities and pedagogy among parents.

Long-Term Parent Engagement - The commitment of parents to quality education is reflected in the long-term engagement they have with schools, with families often choosing institutions for their children’s entire 10 to 12-year academic journey. This fosters stability and supports the growth of schools, ensuring high retention and continual enrolment.
